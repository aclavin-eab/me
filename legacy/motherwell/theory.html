“Precisely what is ‘modern’ in art is its assault on what is dead in immediate experience, and an affirmation of what is alive.”

Evident in Motherwell’s writing, he possessed a deep understanding of modern art, from Dada to Abstract Expressionism. Motherwell also provided valuable insight on subjects such as spirituality, psychology, modernity, and the role of the artist. Much of his theory searched for the true nature of what was human opposed to the assumptions of social culture.

Next

“The ultimate compromise with my father was that if I got a doctorate so that I could teach to support myself the rest would be up to me.”

Motherwell attended a number of prestigious schools in his youth as a member of the upper class, including Harvard and Columbia University. Towards the end of his education his father approached him with the question of what he would do with his life. His response being painting disappointed his father, but under the condition that he would also teach Motherwell was able to pursue art. He went on to teach at Hunter College, Oberlin, and Brown. He was also involved with the renowned Black Mountain College, where his teachings inspried the likes of Cy Twombly, Kenneth Noland, and Robert Rauschenberg.

“And what is modernism, among other things, but the effort to get rid of rhetoric in art?”

The meaning and purpose of modernism is a common theme in Motherwell’s theories. He had serious issues with the state of museums and their power over the relevance of modern art, when museums rarely contain current modern art. At best they had new itirations of what recently was modern art. Motherwell often expressed disgust with the state of other aspects of society from industry to the environment. It could be concluded that he held modern art responsible for ushering into society more appropriate ways of living.

Next

“The modern artist tends to become the last active spiritual being in the great world. It is true that each artist has his own religion.”

Motherwell viewed the artist as a spiritual being living in a material driven world. He held society has turned away form religion through science among other things, but the difference between science and religion was and is major. Religion is a view of the world while science is only a method. Resultingly, religion could not be replaced—it could only be ignored. This yet another issue with society that the modern artist had the duty of shedding light on.

“[Roberto] Matta introduced me to William Baziotes, who in turn introduced me to young Jackson Pollock, Willem de Kooning, and the older Hans Hofmann. Such artists became my real graduate education.”

Motherwell studied at Harvard before transferring to Columbia University, to study under an acclaimed art historian by the name of Meyer Schapiro. Schapiro then introduced Motherwell to Roberto Matta which resulted in Motherwell becoming a part of the New York School of abstract expressionists, of which he was the youngest and the most educated.

Next

“Every intelligent painter carries the whole culture of moder painting in
his head. It is his real subject, of which anything he paints is both a homage and a critique, and anything
he says a gloss.”

Much of Motherwell’s theory revolves around the work of other artists; its importance, flaws, and success. Some of his first published work was on Mondrian and Chirico, and there were few artists contemporary to Motherwell that he did not document some theory about. Pollock and Rothko were both championed by Motherwell's words as well as eulogized. Cy Twombly, a student of Motherwell, was heralded as the best modern young painter Motherwell had come in contact with at the Black Mountain college—quite the esteem considering Motherwell's wide circle of artists. It would seem that Motherwell was able to articulate theories on his contemporaries work than those artists themselves.
